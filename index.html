What you need to know

The MAHA Primer

MAHA — Make America Healthy Again — emerged as a political brand under the Trump administration, attached to a set of genuine public health concerns: chronic disease, ultra-processed foods, synthetic food additives, pesticide exposure, and corporate capture of regulatory agencies like the FDA and USDA.

Whether or not you share any sympathy for its political packaging, these concerns are legitimate and well-documented. Americans consume more ultra-processed food than almost any nation on earth. Food dyes banned in the EU for decades remain common in U.S. products. PFAS contamination affects over 200 million people's drinking water. Childhood obesity and diet-related illness are undeniable crises.

This site tracks those specific policy issues — enacted laws, signed regulations, and executive actions — not the broader MAGA political agenda. We follow what passes and who passes it, regardless of party affiliation.

The state-level data is illuminating. Democratic-led states — California, Maine, Washington, Minnesota — are enacting the most concrete food safety protections in the country. Republican administrations are rolling back EPA rules and deregulating factory farms. A few red-state legislators are crossing lines on food dyes and pesticides. Bipartisan support for these specific issues is real.

But at the federal level, the Democratic Party's record is deeply sobering. Leadership has repeatedly failed to offer a coherent, forceful health regulatory agenda — deferring to corporate interests, defending failing institutions rather than proposing bold reforms, and leaving one of the most obvious electoral openings in years almost entirely unclaimed. After a major Trump executive order weakening food and drug oversight, only Senators Cory Booker and Ed Markey introduced meaningful legislation (the Ensuring Safe and Toxic-Free Foods Act). Such initiatives remain rare exceptions, not a strategy. The political will to challenge corporate food and chemical power has been largely absent at the federal level — from both parties.

NOV 2025

EPA Approves Isocycloseram — A PFAS Pesticide

Trump EPA approved isocycloseram for use on food crops (oranges, tomatoes, almonds, peas, oats), golf courses, and lawns — despite being a PFAS "forever chemical" that breaks down into 40 additional PFAS compounds. Linked to reduced sperm count, smaller testicle size, and liver toxicity. The approval omitted a child-safety buffer despite children's heightened sensitivity. Bees near treated fields may be exposed to 1,500 times the lethal dose. The second PFAS pesticide approved by this EPA within two weeks.

Trump Admin (R) Executive Action Industry-aligned
FEB 2026

EPA Reapproves Dicamba With Weakened Restrictions

EPA reapproved dicamba — described by experts as causing "the worst drift of any herbicide in the history of U.S. agriculture" — with fewer protections than previous versions. Eliminated seasonal cutoff dates and temperature-based spray restrictions that had limited its spread. Since 2016, dicamba drift has damaged millions of acres of crops, orchards, vegetable farms, and wildlife refuges. Threatens monarch butterflies and rusty patched bumblebees. Federal courts had struck down two prior EPA approvals (2020, 2024) before this third approval.

Trump Admin (R) Executive Action Courts struck down twice before
FEB 2026

Mercury & Air Toxics Standards (MATS) Rollback

Trump EPA reversed Biden-era tightening of the Mercury and Air Toxics Standards — the rule governing emissions from coal-fired power plants, the largest human source of mercury pollution. Coal plant mercury enters the food chain through fish and other food sources. Mercury is a neurotoxin that impairs brain development in young children and contributes to heart attacks in adults. EPA cited "hundreds of millions in savings" for utility operators. Announced at a coal plant on the Ohio River in Louisville, Kentucky.

Trump Admin (R) Party-Line

Thomas Massie's No Immunity for Glyphosate Act, co-sponsored by Pingree, Boebert, Mace, and Khanna, would restore victims' right to sue — but has not advanced to a floor vote.

The electoral opening

The Missed Mandate

The public is ahead of Washington on chemical safety, toxic food, and environmental health. A 2026 Pew survey shows the politics are unambiguous. The question is whether Democrats will actually claim this terrain — or keep ceding it.

The political opening has rarely been clearer. A February 2026 Pew Research survey found that more than 70% of Americans are very or somewhat concerned about their own or their loved ones' exposure to harmful chemicals in food and drinking water — with majorities also concerned about chemicals in food packaging, children's products, and farmland. Roughly five in six Americans say government and businesses should do more to ensure chemical safety. That includes nearly eight in ten Republicans. Anxiety about pesticides, food additives, PFAS, and ultra-processed food is not fringe. It is the dominant public sentiment — and it is sitting there, largely unclaimed by federal Democrats.

MAHA has dominated the conversation, but it is riddled with contradiction. Its supporters successfully blocked a Bayer glyphosate liability shield from an appropriations rider — one of the movement's genuine wins. Yet the administration they helped elect has done the opposite at every turn: Republican-led states passed liability shields making it harder for cancer victims to sue Roundup's manufacturer; Trump's DOJ filed a brief urging the Supreme Court to side with Bayer; and the EPA has launched a deregulatory wave against the very protections MAHA's base said it wanted. The protections MAHA supporters are demanding can only come from the kind of robust public infrastructure their political allies are actively destroying.

Meanwhile, federal Democratic leadership has almost entirely failed to capitalize on this. After a major Trump executive order weakening food and drug oversight, only Senators Booker and Markey introduced meaningful counter-legislation. The Green New Deal — despite its merits — is received as a climate framework; for many families, climate feels temporally distant while chemical exposure feels immediate: a child's asthma, a neighbor's cancer, a contaminated well. Democrats have largely failed to speak in those everyday terms. The failure to connect environmental and chemical policy to immediate lived experience has been a costly political and moral mistake.

The state-level record shows what is possible and what the mandate looks like in practice. Maine became the first state to pass a sweeping PFAS phase-out law, requiring product reporting and moving toward broad prohibitions. San Francisco's City Attorney sued Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, and Kraft Heinz, arguing they knowingly engineered addictive ultra-processed foods and should bear liability for the resulting disease epidemics. New York restricted neonicotinoid pesticides linked to pollinator collapse. Connecticut strengthened habitability protections for mold exposure. These are real, enforceable interventions — not brand management.

Democrats can make the case that the people who jumped to MAHA were not wrong to seek change. They were wrong about who would deliver it. But only if Democrats are willing to actually take on corporate power — not just defend the pre-Trump status quo, which wasn't adequate either.

What Reclaiming This Terrain Requires

A real democratic health movement would start from where the public already is. It would demand stronger regulatory agencies and robust enforcement — not liability shields for pesticide manufacturers, regardless of which party advances them. It would treat chemical safety as a matter of shared material conditions, not a culture-war wedge. It would connect climate and environmental health in language people recognize from their own lives. It would reject the use of "health" to target marginalized communities. Everyone drinks water. Everyone eats. Children across political lines attend public schools. The body is not partisan. The 2026 midterms are an opportunity to prove it — but only if Democrats are willing to challenge the corporate interests that have blocked progress on both sides of the aisle for decades.

Progressive Roots of Food & Environmental Health

1906
Pure Food and Drug Act

Progressive-era legislation banning adulterated foods — the original push to regulate what corporations put in food.

1962
Rachel Carson — Silent Spring

Documented pesticide harms and industry capture of regulators. Led directly to DDT ban and creation of the EPA.

1971
Alice Waters & Farm-to-Table

Launched a cultural shift toward organic, local, seasonal food — a progressive food ethic before it had a name.

1991
Environmental Justice Movement

Communities of color documented disproportionate exposure to pesticides, toxic food deserts, and industrial pollution — issues MAHA never meaningfully addresses.

2006
Michael Pollan — The Omnivore's Dilemma

Mainstream reckoning with corporate food systems and ultra-processed food — a progressive cultural touchstone.

2010
Michelle Obama — Let's Move!

Federal push to reform school nutrition, combat childhood obesity, and improve USDA meal standards. Real food policy progress.

2023–25
State Food Safety Wave

CA, WA, MN, IL pass the food dye, PFAS, and nutrition reforms that MAHA claims to want — enacted by Democratic-led governments.
